The problem

Most SaaS products treat error states, validation messages, and empty states as afterthoughts: copy written at the end of a sprint, usually by an engineer, usually in the form of "Something went wrong. Please try again."

That copy fails users in the moments they most need clarity: when they've hit a wall, made a mistake, or are new and don't yet know what the product does. For a field operations tool whose users are managing real-world work on a deadline, vague error copy isn't just frustrating. It's a productivity cost.

This sample demonstrates how intentional UX writing turns those three pattern types into moments of trust rather than friction.

Empty states: answer "why" before "what"

Empty states have two jobs. The first is obvious: tell the user the list is empty. The second is less obvious: give them a reason to care about filling it. Most empty states skip the second job.

The Fieldwork empty states lead with benefit before CTA. "Projects help your team track field work, assign tasks, and keep everyone on the same page" answers the question a new user is actually asking (why should I do this?) before asking them to act.

Three distinct variants because the scenario matters. First use needs to motivate action. Zero state needs to affirm rather than prompt. No results needs to offer recovery paths. The same copy doesn't work for all three.

Form validation: say what to do, not what went wrong

Standard validation messages name the error: "Invalid input," "Required field," "Incorrect format." These describe the problem from the system's perspective, not the user's.

Every validation message in this sample is written as an instruction, not a diagnosis. "Letters, numbers, and hyphens only." Not "Invalid characters." "A project lead is required before publishing." Not "Required field." The second example includes the consequence ("before publishing") so the user understands the system's logic rather than fighting an unexplained rule.

Two additional decisions: the character count warning appears at 87% capacity, not 100%, so users can edit before they're stopped. The error count ("Fix 3 errors before publishing") appears near the submit button so users don't scroll to understand why the form is still blocked.

Error states: three sentences, three jobs

Every error state follows the same structure: name the failure, provide context or reassurance, give a recovery path. "Changes couldn't be saved. Your session expired while you were editing. Your draft is safe. Sign back in to continue." Three moves. Each carries new information.

No apologies. "We're sorry for the inconvenience" adds no information and undermines the calm, professional tone field operations teams need. They need resolution, not sympathy.

The destructive confirmation modal applies different rules. The headline uses the object name rather than a generic "are you sure?" Specific consequences ("47 tasks") stop confident users from confirming by habit. "Keep project" not "Cancel" — positive-framed, unambiguous, describes the outcome rather than the action.

The copy system

The sample includes a copy spec documenting voice, tone by context, and six named rules with right/wrong examples. A UX writing sample that only shows finished copy is incomplete. The spec shows the system behind the copy: rules a team could apply consistently without asking the UX writer to review every string.

That's the difference between writing good copy and building a writing practice.

What I'd push further

Empty state body copy. Benefit-led language tests well in onboarding contexts, but field operations users are experienced professionals who may find it condescending. I'd A/B test a shorter, more direct variant against the warmer version before committing.

Error count position. I placed it near the submit button assuming users scroll before re-submitting. If analytics showed users re-submitting without scrolling, I'd move it to a sticky banner at the top of the form instead.
