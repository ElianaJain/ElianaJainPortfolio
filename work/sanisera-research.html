Research Documentation  ·  Mortuary Archaeology

Burial, Identity, and the Post-Roman World

How the arrival of Christianity reshaped burial practice for women in 5th to 7th century Menorca, Spain

Researcher Eliana Lucia De La Rosa Pichardo
Site Necropolis 6, Sanisera, Menorca
Period 5th to 7th Century AD
Artifacts 93 recovered across 14 tombs
Funded by UW La Crosse Undergraduate Research Grant
The Question
How did early Christianity change burial rituals for women in post-Roman Menorca?
The Challenge
Fragmentary, disarticulated remains in looted, acid-soil tombs with a sample too small for statistical analysis
The Finding
Christianity aligned with existing Germanic burial practice rather than replacing it. Rome was the cultural outlier
01   Context

A small island at the center of everything

Menorca is one of the most layered archaeological sites in the Mediterranean. From Talayotic megalith builders in the Neolithic through Carthaginian mercenary recruitment, Roman military occupation, Germanic Visigothic settlement, and early Christian conversion. The Balearic Islands absorbed every major Mediterranean civilization in sequence. Sanisera, a Roman port city on Menorca's northern coast, contains a necropolis that holds the physical residue of that entire transition.

My research focused on the necropolis during the 5th to 7th centuries AD, the period after Rome's western empire collapsed and before the Moorish conquest of AD 711. This is the Dark Ages in the most literal historiographical sense: a period so poorly documented that most historians skip from Antiquity directly to the Medieval. I was interested in what the burials themselves could tell us about what actually happened to the people living through that transition, specifically the women.

Documentation is the thing that outlasts the system it describes. The tombs of Sanisera are documentation. My job was to read them carefully and say honestly what they did and did not show.

Research reflection, 2011
02   Constraints

Working with incomplete evidence

The Necropolis 6 site presented overlapping preservation challenges that made straightforward analysis impossible. The evidence was fragmentary at every level: physical, stratigraphic, and archival. Working within those constraints without overclaiming became the central methodological challenge.

⚗️
Acidic soil composition
The clay and sand mixture at the site did not allow proper skeletal preservation. Most remains were fractured, fragmentary, or deteriorated beyond reliable sex determination.
🏺
Looted and reused tombs
Evidence of historical looting disturbed stratigraphic layers throughout. Tombs were also intentionally reused. Previous burials moved aside to make room for new ones, mixing individuals across time periods.
📊
Sample too small for statistics
Only 93 artifacts recovered across 14 tombs. Statistical pattern analysis was not possible. All findings had to be framed as comparative and speculative rather than definitive.
📋
Incomplete archival records
No stratigraphy records existed for the 2008 excavation season, making it impossible to match artifacts to tomb context for that year's finds.

These constraints shaped the entire research approach. Rather than drawing firm conclusions, the methodology relied on comparative analysis by cross-referencing Sanisera artifacts against necropolises in Andalucia, southern Germany, and France to identify cultural patterns that could survive the evidentiary gaps.

03   Historical Context

The burial question was never simple

The central finding emerged from understanding what each culture actually did with its dead before Christianity arrived.

Talayotic / Pre-Roman
Communal megalithic burial
The indigenous Talayotic people of Menorca buried their dead communally in stone structures. The concept of inhumation, placing the body whole in the ground, was already established practice.
Roman Period
Cremation as the norm
Rome cremated its dead and placed remains in urns in catacombs. Inhumation only became common practice in the Roman world from the second century AD onward, relatively late in Roman history.
Germanic / Visigothic
Inhumation with grave goods
The Visigoths who settled Hispania already practiced inhumation and buried their dead with personal possessions: jewelry, weapons, and tools, reflecting status and identity carried into the afterlife.
Early Christian
Inhumation aligned with Germanic practice
Early Christianity required inhumation. The body had to be kept intact for the Second Coming. This aligned directly with existing Germanic burial tradition. Christianity did not disrupt local practice. It formalized it.
04   Key Finding

Rome was the outlier

Research Finding

Early Christianity reinforced Germanic burial practice in post-Roman Menorca rather than replacing it. The Germanic peoples already buried their dead whole with grave goods. Christian doctrine requiring bodily preservation for resurrection aligned naturally with what they were already doing. Rome, with its tradition of cremation, was the cultural outlier in this transition. The women buried at Sanisera with bronze jewelry, silver earrings, and glass paste beads were not departing from Christian practice. They were expressing a layered identity in which Germanic tradition, Roman influence, and early Christian belief had become inseparable.

The artifacts themselves confirmed this layering. Bronze earrings matching Visigothic styles found in Andalucia. Glass eye beads with Phoenician origins reused as jewelry in Late Antiquity. Two coins dated to Constantius II and Late Antiquity. A bronze ring engraved with a cross and astral symbols. Christian iconography alongside pre-Christian motifs in the same object, in the same tomb.

The tombs were communal, reused across generations, oriented west-east in the Christian manner, but filled with the accumulated material culture of every civilization that had passed through Menorca. The women buried there were not choosing between identities. They were living all of them simultaneously.

05   What This Research Required

Skills that transferred

Evidence vs. interpretation
Distinguishing between what the record actually showed and what could be reasonably inferred from incomplete data
Comparative analysis
Cross-referencing multiple source bodies to identify patterns that survive evidentiary gaps
Honest uncertainty
Framing findings as speculative where the sample size demanded it, without undermining the value of the analysis
Systems thinking
Reading individual artifacts as data points within a larger cultural system rather than isolated objects
Primary research
Field excavation, artifact cataloguing, database analysis in Microsoft Access, and cross-institutional source work
Audience-aware writing
Translating archaeological methodology and Latin terminology into clear academic prose for a non-specialist committee
Why This Belongs in a Technical Writing Portfolio

The habits of mind developed in this research: reading systems through incomplete records, distinguishing evidence from interpretation, documenting what a record shows and what it does not, treating documentation itself as the artifact that outlasts the context it describes. These are the same habits that drive my approach to AI documentation, API reference work, and content governance. The subject matter changed from skeletal remains to software systems. The epistemological framework did not.
