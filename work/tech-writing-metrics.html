The Problem With Page Views

Most documentation teams default to page views as their primary metric. It's easy to measure, easy to report, and almost entirely useless as a signal of documentation quality.

High page views might mean your content is valuable. It might also mean it's confusing and users keep returning because it never resolves their question. Low page views might mean nobody needs that content. Or nobody can find it. The number alone tells you nothing about whether the documentation is working.

Effective documentation metrics measure outcomes, not activity. The question is never "did people read it." The question is "did people do the thing differently after reading it."

Weak metrics
✗Page views and unique visitors
✗Time on page in isolation
✗Number of documents published
✗Word count or page count
✗"Documentation is complete"
Strong metrics
✓Support ticket reduction by topic
✓Task completion rate after reading
✓Tool adoption rate post-launch
✓Return visit rate to same article
✓Onboarding time reduction

The Three-Level Framework

Effective documentation metrics operate at three levels. Each level answers a different question and requires different data sources. All three are necessary for a complete picture.

01
Findability
Can they locate it?
Users can't benefit from documentation they can't find. Findability metrics reveal gaps in content architecture and search optimization.
  • Search terms with zero results
  • Time to first click from search
  • Navigation paths to content
  • Dead end pages with no next action
  • Failed search rate
02
Effectiveness
Does it actually help?
Finding the content is necessary but not sufficient. Effectiveness metrics measure whether users get what they need from the content once they find it.
  • Task completion rate after reading
  • Return visit rate to same article
  • Support ticket volume by topic
  • Time on page in context
  • User feedback scores
03
Impact
Is it changing behavior?
The highest level of measurement. Impact metrics connect documentation directly to business outcomes and behavioral change at scale.
  • Tool adoption rate post-launch
  • Support escalation reduction
  • Onboarding time reduction
  • Error rate reduction in workflows
  • Employee proficiency scores
03

Metrics in Detail

Each metric category requires different tools, different stakeholders, and different interpretive frameworks. Here's how to apply them in practice.

Level 01 · Findability
Search and Navigation
Search data reveals where content architecture fails users before they even reach the content.
  • Zero-result searches identify content gaps. These are terms users search for that return nothing
  • High bounce on search results pages means results aren't matching intent
  • Navigation path analysis shows whether users reach content through intended routes
  • Dead end pages with no outbound clicks suggest content that doesn't resolve the question
Level 02 · Effectiveness
Content Performance
Effectiveness metrics reveal whether content resolves the user's need or sends them elsewhere.
  • Return visits to the same article within a session suggest the content isn't resolving the issue
  • Support ticket volume by topic: track before and after documentation updates
  • Time on page interpreted in context. Short may mean users gave up. Long may mean confusion.
  • Thumbs up or down feedback where available gives direct user signal
Level 03 · Impact
Behavioral Change
Impact metrics connect documentation directly to business outcomes and user proficiency at scale.
  • Tool adoption rate before and after documentation launch
  • Support escalation reduction over time by product area
  • Onboarding time reduction for new users following documented workflows
  • Error rate reduction in tasks covered by documentation
Both levels · Qualitative
Feedback Signals
Quantitative metrics tell you what happened. Qualitative signals tell you why. Both are necessary.
  • Support team input on which topics generate the most questions
  • Stakeholder feedback during documentation reviews
  • User interviews and usability testing observations
  • Comments on documentation pages where available

Applied at SAP Ariba

At SAP Ariba I applied elements of this framework to documentation serving 2,000+ enterprise users across procurement and supply chain workflows. Two approaches shaped how I measured and improved content effectiveness.

WalkMe Integration
I participated in WalkMe integration training at SAP Ariba as the team prepared to track how employees navigated documentation and where they encountered friction. WalkMe enabled tracking of page visits, navigation paths, drop-off points, and user journey patterns within the help system. The goal was to move from anecdotal feedback to behavioral data. The goal was to understand not just what users said about the documentation but what they actually did with it.

Support ticket analysis

I worked closely with support teams to identify which documentation topics generated the highest volume of support escalations. High ticket volume on a documented topic was a signal that the existing content wasn't resolving the user's question. The content may have been incomplete, inaccurate, hard to find, or structured in a way that didn't match how users thought about the problem.

When I updated documentation based on support ticket patterns, I tracked whether ticket volume on those topics decreased in the weeks following. Decrease confirmed the documentation was working. Continued high volume signaled the content needed further revision.

The north star metric

Across both quantitative and qualitative approaches, the metric that mattered most was behavior change. If employees were using internal tools more confidently, making fewer errors, and contacting support less frequently after a documentation update, the documentation worked. If behavior didn't change, the content needed to change.

North Star
The question is never "did people read it." The question is "did people do the thing differently after reading it."

Metrics Readiness Checklist

Before launching documentation, establish your measurement baseline. Before reporting on documentation performance, verify your metrics are measuring outcomes rather than activity.

□Have you defined what behavioral change this documentation should produce?
□Do you have a baseline support ticket volume for this topic before the documentation ships?
□Is search tracking enabled to identify zero-result queries?
□Can you track task completion rate or tool adoption rate for the documented workflow?
□Do you have a qualitative feedback channel: user surveys, support team input, or direct user access?
□Have you identified which stakeholders will receive metrics reports and at what cadence?
□Is there a review trigger: a metric threshold that signals content needs updating?
□Are you measuring outcomes rather than activity: behavior change rather than page views?
